# 1664 · bytemill

**The one question:** What if a Resonance instrument were pure algorithmic grit —
8-bit "bytebeat" music synthesised directly from a single time-counter formula,
that you bend and mutate live with your keyboard?

This is deliberately the opposite of a clean consonant tone. Bytebeat is harsh,
aliased and glitchy by construction, and that harshness is the instrument.

## What bytebeat is

Bytebeat is raw PCM audio generated by evaluating a short integer expression of a
running sample counter `t`, for example:

```
t*((t>>12|t>>8)&63&t>>4)
```

The expression is evaluated once per audio "tick", masked to a single byte
(`& 255`, i.e. 0..255), and that byte is mapped to `-1..1` for Web Audio. The low
bits of `t` produce audible pitch and rhythm; the bitwise operators (`>>`, `|`,
`&`) give bytebeat its characteristic aliased, glitchy grain. The classic
"sample rate" is **8000 Hz** — `t` is advanced ~8000 times/second regardless of
the audio hardware's real sample rate, which is where the lo-fi aliasing comes
from.

### Named reference

Bytebeat was discovered and named by **Viznut (Ville-Matias Heikkilä) in 2011**,
in "Discovering the beauty of new-school algorithmic music". Modern browser
composers in the same lineage include **bytebeat.cloud** and **ByteBeats.art**
(dadabots).

## How this prototype works

- **Synthesis** happens in an `AudioWorkletNode`. The processor source is an
  inline string in `engine.ts`, turned into a **Blob URL**
  (`URL.createObjectURL(new Blob([src], {type:"application/javascript"}))`) and
  loaded with `audioContext.audioWorklet.addModule(blobUrl)`. Nothing is served
  from `/public`; the whole engine is self-contained.
- The worklet keeps a fractional accumulator: each output sample it adds
  `tRate / sampleRate`; every time the accumulator crosses 1 it advances `t` by
  one and recomputes the byte, **holding the last byte between ticks**
  (sample-and-hold). That is what produces authentic bytebeat aliasing at a
  chosen t-rate that is independent of the hardware sample rate.
- The current formula, the constant `k`, and the t-rate are sent to the worklet
  via `port.postMessage`. The formula is compiled once with `new Function` from
  the **curated library only** (the visitor picks a formula and a numeric `k`;
  they cannot inject arbitrary code).
- The worklet posts byte snapshots back to the main thread ~46×/s to drive the
  visuals.
- **Audio safety chain:** worklet → soft-clip `WaveShaper` (tanh) →
  `DynamicsCompressor` → master `GainNode` at 0.11 (below the 0.12 ceiling) →
  destination. Bytebeat can be brutally loud, so it is tamed before the speakers.

## Visuals (Canvas2D)

- A CRT **oscilloscope** of the live waveform, with green glow, a baseline, and
  scanlines.
- A scrolling **bitfield**: the low byte's 8 bits are rendered as a running
  column texture, so you literally see the bit patterns that make the rhythm
  (LSB at the bottom). Newest samples enter at the right.

Both respect `prefers-reduced-motion`: glow, scanlines and CRT persistence are
dropped and the animation is throttled.

## Controls

- **1–8** — select one of eight named formulas.
- **← / →** or **[** / **]** — bend the t-rate (2000–16000 Hz): tempo and pitch.
- **↑ / ↓** or **−** / **=** — mutate the arithmetic constant **k** that is
  live-substituted into the formula. The highlighted `k` in the on-screen formula
  is what you are bending; each formula clamps `k` to a useful range.
- **Space** — play / pause.

On-screen buttons mirror every key for touch, but the keyboard is the primary
interface.

## Auto-demo (ghost performance)

On the first gesture (the Play button) the piece starts a **deterministic** ghost
performance: it cycles the formula library on a fixed schedule and bends `k` and
the t-rate with sines of `audioContext.currentTime`, so the machine is never
silent or blank when reviewed unattended. Any real keypress takes over from the
ghost instantly. There is no `Math.random`, `Date.now` or `new Date` in the
executable code — all variation is derived from `currentTime` and fixed
constants.

## Known limitations

- `AudioWorklet` is required. If it (or Web Audio) is unavailable, the piece
  shows a readable notice instead of playing.
- Byte snapshots are transferred ~46×/s, so the oscilloscope and bitfield show a
  faithful but slightly time-quantised view of the audio, not a per-sample scope.
- The compressor + soft-clip tame dynamics for safety; a purist raw-bytebeat
  player would sound a touch harsher and louder than this deliberately does.
- Formulas are compiled with `new Function` inside the worklet; a strict CSP that
  forbids `unsafe-eval` in worklet scope would block synthesis.
