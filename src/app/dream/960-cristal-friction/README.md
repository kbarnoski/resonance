# 960 · Cristal Friction

**One line:** Bow glowing rods of glass and metal with your cursor — the singing comes from a real stick-slip friction physical model, not a sampled emulation.

## The question

> What if you could BOW glass and metal with your cursor — and the singing
> comes from real friction physics, not a sampled emulation?

An adult, meditative friction-bowed resonator instrument in the lineage of the
**Cristal Baschet** and the **glass armonica**. Drag the pointer across a row of
luminous resonators: the **cursor velocity is the bow speed** and the
**vertical press (how low on a rod you drag) is the bow force**. The tone is
generated by a real **friction stick-slip physical model** coupled to a tuned
modal resonator bank.

## How to use

1. Open the prototype and press **"Light the rods & bow"** (Web Audio needs a
   user gesture before it can start).
2. A gentle ~2-second auto-bow sweeps across two rods so the screen sings on its
   own within a moment. **Move the pointer and the auto-demo stops** — you take
   over.
3. **Drag slowly and steadily** for pure singing tones.
4. **Drag fast and light (high up a rod)** and the model thins toward whistling,
   raucous "wolf" tones — a feature, exactly like over-driving a real bow.
5. **Drag lower on a rod** to press harder (more bow force → fuller tone).

The six rods are tuned to a **just-intoned pentatonic + octave** drone on ~D3,
so any combination is consonant. The idea on display is the **friction timbre
and the embodied bowing gesture**, not a harmony engine.

## What is actually happening (the friction model)

The exciter is a **real elasto-plastic / LuGre stick-slip friction model**,
solved per audio sample inside an **AudioWorkletProcessor** (sample-accurate,
off the main thread). Each rod is:

- a **modal resonator body** — a small bank of Chamberlin state-variable
  band-pass resonators tuned to the rod's fundamental plus a few **mildly
  inharmonic partials** (glass/metal rods stretch their overtones slightly),
  each with its own Q so glassier rods ring longer; and
- a **friction exciter** coupled to that body's contact **velocity**.

Every sample the processor:

1. reads the body's contact velocity `vBody` (the sum of the modal band-pass
   outputs);
2. forms the **relative velocity** between bow and body, `vRel = vBody − vbow`;
3. passes `vRel` through an **elasto-plastic bristle law**: a bristle deflection
   `z` bends under **stiction** when `vRel ≈ 0` (the "stick", where the rod is
   carried along with the bow) and **breaks loose and flows** once the relative
   velocity crosses the Stribeck threshold (the "slip", where kinetic friction
   drops below static friction). The transition between stick and slip is the
   smooth elasto-plastic `alpha` ramp;
4. injects the resulting **friction force** (scaled by bow normal force) back
   into the velocity integrator of every mode.

Feeding the resonator's own velocity through the friction nonlinearity and back
into itself is what **self-sustains the Helmholtz stick-slip oscillation** — the
alternating sticking/slipping that makes a bowed string (or a bowed glass rod)
sing. This is the friction interaction described by McIntyre, Schumacher &
Woodhouse (1983). The model is genuinely self-oscillating: it is silent at rest,
locks to the rod's pitch under a steady bow, and is bounded and stable.

**Honest note on the amplitude shaping.** Once a real bowed string enters the
Helmholtz regime its limit-cycle amplitude is fairly constant; this model
behaves the same way (its self-oscillation settles to a roughly constant
amplitude). So the gestural *dynamics* — how loud and how present a rod sounds —
are imposed on the model's output: **bow speed sets loudness** and very
light/fast bowing thins the voice toward whistling. The pitch, the rich
stick-slip waveform, the self-oscillation, the silence-at-rest, the pitch-lock
and the threshold-to-sing are all produced by the friction physics itself; the
loudness/brightness envelope is a deliberate, low-risk shaping layer on top so
the instrument stays expressive and stable without the headless-tuning risk of
an unbounded continuous-amplitude friction solve.

The master chain is `gain ≤ 0.22 → lowpass 7.2 kHz → DynamicsCompressor`, and
both the per-rod output and the bus are soft-clamped/`tanh`-saturated, so the
instrument can never get harsh or blow up.

### Audio architecture

- The AudioWorklet source is kept as a **string** (`worklet-source.ts`) and
  loaded via a **Blob URL** (`URL.createObjectURL`) into
  `audioContext.audioWorklet.addModule(...)`, so there is no `public/` file to
  ship.
- If `audioWorklet` is unavailable, it falls back to a per-rod **sawtooth →
  resonant lowpass** oscillator voice (rose notice shown) so it still sings.

### Visuals

- A hand-written raw **WebGL2** fullscreen fragment shader draws N vertical
  glowing rods. Each rod's width, glow and a **Chladni-like standing-wave
  shimmer** are driven by that rod's live amplitude, posted ~per-1024-samples
  from the worklet. The pointer leaves a brief **friction-spark trail**.
- If WebGL2 is unavailable it degrades to a **Canvas2D** rod view (rose notice
  shown).

## Named references

- **McIntyre, Schumacher & Woodhouse**, *"On the oscillations of musical
  instruments"*, J. Acoust. Soc. Am. (1983) — the bowed-string friction
  interaction and Helmholtz motion this model follows.
- The **elasto-plastic friction model for bowed strings** — a passive,
  real-time stick-slip (LuGre/bristle) formulation; part of the 2025–2026
  physical-modeling-synthesis revival (e.g. *Frontiers in Signal Processing*
  2025 editorial, "Sound synthesis through physical modeling").
- The **Cristal Baschet** (Bernard & François Baschet) and Benjamin Franklin's
  **glass armonica** (1761) — the metal-and-glass bowed/rubbed-resonator
  instrument lineage.

## Unverified / build-checked only

There is **no GPU or audio device in the build environment**, so this prototype
is **build-checked, not heard or seen**:

- The TypeScript types and ESLint pass cleanly for this folder.
- The AudioWorklet source string was extracted and **run in Node** with stubbed
  worklet globals: it parses, registers, is silent at rest, self-oscillates at
  the rod pitch (~199 Hz for a 200 Hz rod), tracks bow speed for loudness, and
  stays bounded (worst case: all six rods bowed at maximum → bus peak ≈ 0.85,
  before the 0.22 master gain). See the build notes in the parent report.
- The **WebGL2 shader and Canvas2D fallback have not been rendered**; they are
  reasoned-through but not visually confirmed.
- Exact *sound quality* (how musical the wolf tones are, how pleasant the
  shimmer looks) is a subjective call that needs a real device to judge.
